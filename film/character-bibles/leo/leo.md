## Why this bible exists

Leo is the emotional center of the film.

He must stay visually and emotionally stable across:
- poverty scenes
- memory scenes
- violence
- dream imagery
- close-up acting shots

If his face, age-read, clothing logic, or emotional read drift too much, the film loses cohesion fast.

## On-screen purpose

In early Act 1, Leo should read as:
- hungry
- isolated
- observant
- ashamed
- grieving
- more emotionally open than stoic
- physically vulnerable, but not weak-willed

He should not read as:
- a polished fantasy hero
- visibly chosen or mythic too early
- cocky
- emotionally numb
- generically rebellious

## Age and physical read

Target screen read:
- about 16
- slight build from deprivation, not from stylized fashion-thinness
- young face, but not childlike
- tired eyes
- physical awkwardness in the present-day body language

Important distinction:
- Leo's underlying personal look in Act 1 should already be recognizably the same person as the teen concept-art version
- the difference is not a different identity or face design
- the difference is his earlier material state, wardrobe, and present-life circumstance

He should feel like someone who has grown up with:
- too little food
- too much stress
- too little social ease

## Face and expression

Preferred read:
- expressive eyes
- guarded but not blank
- sadness close to the surface
- alertness when threatened
- flashes of warmth only in memory-linked moments

Expression range needed for current Act 1:
- waking panic
- quiet embarrassment
- gratitude held back by uncertainty
- fear during the public attack
- hunger and exhaustion
- dread around Marshe
- humiliation after the beating
- frustration during training
- devastation at losing the jewelry box
- cautious curiosity in Richard's workshop

## Hair

Keep hair:
- practical
- slightly unkempt
- believable for poverty
- not stylish in a modern way

Avoid:
- elaborate fantasy grooming
- overly clean or salon-shaped silhouettes
- hair that makes him read older or more glamorous

## Costume logic

Current Act 1 Leo should have:
- simple worn village clothes
- muted earth or weathered neutral tones
- visible age and reuse in fabric
- practical layering if weather calls for it

His clothes should suggest:
- poverty
- repetition
- a life without vanity or abundance

Avoid:
- ornate fantasy tailoring
- heroic leather-adventurer styling
- flashy color contrast
- clothing that feels royal, noble, or prophecy-coded

Clarification:
- Act 1 clothing should read more like a poor village boy's clothing than the later teen concept-art outfits
- later teen concept art can still guide Leo's underlying face, hair, age read, and overall identity
- for early film work, keep the person and lose the later-story gear language

## Body language and performance direction

Present-day Leo:
- moves carefully in public
- takes up little space when socially pressured
- speeds up only when fear or urgency takes over
- hesitates before choices that touch grief
- fights badly when imitating his father

Memory-facing Leo:
- should feel younger, safer, softer

Acting note:
- his emotional life is active even when he is quiet
- silence should feel like pressure, not emptiness

## Relationship reads in current scope

Around Richard:
- wary
- uncertain
- faintly pulled toward him without trust yet

Around Marshe:
- old fear
- humiliation
- instinct to avoid escalation

Around his father's memory:
- reverence
- longing
- inadequacy

Around his mother-linked objects:
- tenderness
- protectiveness
- emotional destabilization

## Visual rules for current production

Leo should usually be framed as:
- isolated in space
- pressed by architecture
- small against the village
- intimate in close-up when grief or fear rises

His visual world should emphasize:
- hands
- breath
- eyes
- worn clothing texture
- mud, rain, wood, and low light around him

## Continuity anchors

The following should remain stable across generations unless intentionally revised:
- approximate age read
- face shape
- hair silhouette
- clothing silhouette
- overall poverty level
- emotional vulnerability

## Required variants for production

At minimum, Leo needs these variants:

### Variant A: present-day village Leo

Use for:
- home scenes
- market scenes
- chase scenes
- workshop reveal

Design note:
- this is the main Act 1 hybrid version
- same core personal look as the teenager concept art
- poorer, simpler, more village-appropriate clothing closer to his actual circumstance

### Variant B: injured Act 1 Leo

Use for:
- post-attack and post-beating scenes
- later Act 1 close-ups after visible harm

Visible changes:
- facial cut or bruising as needed
- more exhausted physical read

### Variant C: warm-memory Leo

Use for:
- mother memory
- family market memory
- younger stair-observer memory if needed

Differences:
- softer emotional read
- slightly safer posture
- warmer lighting context

Design note:
- this variant can borrow some softness from the child references without making him literally read as much younger in every memory
- use the child references mainly to preserve tenderness and emotional openness

## Non-negotiables

- Leo must not look too heroic too early.
- He must not look polished or fashionable.
- He must feel specific, not like a generic fantasy boy.
- His vulnerability must remain visible even when he is determined.
- His memory versions should feel warmer without becoming sentimental fantasy gloss.

## Avoid drift

Do not let generations drift toward:
- model-pretty young prince
- anime hero proportions
- grimdark adult mercenary
- modern teen styling
- high-fantasy chosen-one costuming
- exaggerated misery caricature

## Prompt-building notes

When prompting for Leo, keep language centered on:
- grounded fantasy
- poor exile-village teenager
- hungry, grieving, observant
- worn clothes
- muted palette
- intimate cinematic framing
- tactile realism

Prompt less around:
- destiny
- royalty
- prophecy
- spectacle

Those ideas belong to the story, but should not dominate his early visual read.

## Approval workflow

Before generating many Leo shots:
1. Lock one primary present-day portrait
2. Lock one full-body reference
3. Lock one injured variant reference
4. Lock one warm-memory reference
5. Reuse those references when generating sequence frames

## Maintenance note

This file should only change when:
- Leo's approved screen look changes
- the production needs a new variant
- a story change affects how Leo must appear on screen

Do not update this file for every beat-level change in Act 1.
