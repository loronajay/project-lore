# Visual Language

## Direction

The film should feel:
- grounded
- severe
- tactile
- intimate
- mournful
- dangerous

Avoid making the world feel glossy, whimsical, or conventionally magical.

## Visual identity

Preferred traits:
- weathered materials
- muted earth tones
- controlled bursts of sacred light
- narrow interiors
- smoke, rain, mud, wood grain, worn cloth, and rough metal
- lighting that feels motivated by the world

## Camera language

Prioritize:
- restrained camera movement
- close emotional framing
- selective wide shots for isolation
- subjective fragments in dream material
- physical readability during violence

Avoid:
- constant floating camera drift
- over-cut action for its own sake
- overly smooth synthetic movement
- fantasy spectacle before emotional grounding

## Character presentation rules

Leo:
- vulnerable first
- not framed as a hero too early
- often isolated in frame

Richard:
- controlled, solid, partly unreadable
- introduced with weight and economy

Marshe:
- threatening through certainty and familiarity
- cruelty should feel targeted, not theatrical

Leo's father in memory:
- warm, capable, emotionally anchoring
- movement should feel skilled but not showy

## Dreams and memory

Dreams should feel:
- fragmentary
- intense
- disorienting
- symbolically charged

Memories should feel:
- warmer than the present
- emotionally specific
- not fully explanatory

The audience should feel that Leo is remembering sensations and fragments, not receiving neat exposition.

## Motion philosophy

Not every shot needs full animation.

Use motion where it matters most:
- eyes
- breathing
- cloth
- flame
- rain
- camera push
- impact moments

Stillness should be part of the style, not a sign of incompleteness.
