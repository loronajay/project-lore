LORE

Written by: Jay Lorona




FADE IN:


EXT. BATTLEFIELD — DAWN (DREAM)

A field of violence.
Bodies in strange armor, design unlike anything ordinary.
The metal clashes in a way that feels wrong for the hour.

LEO stands in the middle of it. He is himself but not himself — his body stands still while everything around it is chaos.
Smoke drifts low across the ground.
The color has been partly drained out of the world.

He looks down. His hands are bloodied.

A sound — not quite sound. A pressure behind the eyes.

He looks up.

INT. LEO'S HOUSE — MORNING

Leo wakes hard. Chest heaving, one hand gripping the edge of the bed. His eyes open on a cracked wooden ceiling.

He lies there. The breathing slows. He stares up.

The room around him settles into focus. Small. Neat. The neatness of someone trying to maintain order over circumstances that don't cooperate.
There is almost nothing here that suggests comfort.

Leo sits up. The dream is already leaving. He lets it.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

He moves through the house like he's looking for something without wanting to find it. Opens a cupboard. Closes it.
Runs his hand along an empty shelf.

He stops at a small chest by the wall. Opens it.

Inside: a jewelry box. Dark wood, small, clearly old. The kind of object that was well-made once and has been kept carefully since.

He picks it up. Holds it.

LEO
(quietly, to no one)
Not this.

He sets it back in the chest and closes the lid.

FLASHBACK — INT. THE SAME HOUSE — YEARS EARLIER

Soft light. Warm. A fire burning somewhere off frame.

A WOMAN sits with her back against the wall, a young child — LEO, maybe three or four — in her lap.
She's humming something. Not a song with words. Just a sound that fills the space between them.

She isn't clearly defined. Her face comes in and out of focus the way things do in very early memory.
The warmth around her is the sharpest detail. Her hands, holding him.
The sound of the hum.

Leo's small hands reach up toward the jewelry box in her hands.

END FLASHBACK.

He looks around the room again. His eyes land on a decorative dish on a high shelf — ornate, out of place with the rest of the house.
He reaches up and takes it down.

He holds it in both hands and looks at it for a moment.

It'll have to do.

He sets down the memory the way he set down the box — gently, deliberately — and moves toward the door.


EXT. VILLAGE — MORNING

The exile village in daylight. Stone buildings close together.
Narrow lanes that don't go anywhere particularly. The market square ahead with its stalls and low chatter.

People moving but not with much energy. A place maintaining itself out of habit more than hope.

Leo walks with the dish under his arm.


EXT. MARKET STALLS — MORNING

Three stalls. Three tries.

First stall: the merchant looks at the dish, turns it over, hands it back. Doesn't speak. Leo moves on.

Second stall: the merchant isn't even curious. He waves Leo away before Leo's finished the sentence.

Third stall: a longer look. The merchant sets it down on the counter and examines it properly.
Leo waits with his hands at his sides.

The merchant shakes his head.

Leo picks up the dish. He walks away from all three stalls and sits against the wall of the alley that borders the market.
He holds the dish across his knees.

He looks at it. Useless, fancy thing.

He sits.


FLASHBACK — INT. LEO'S HOUSE — YEARS EARLIER

A door is slightly open. Inside, barely visible: LEO'S FATHER in his chair. Pale. Thinner than he was.
His eyes are open but there is a quality to the openness that doesn't look restful.
He is awake the way a person is awake when sleep has stopped being available to them.

Young LEO, maybe twelve, stands at the crack of the door.

YOUNG LEO
Papa.

A pause. The man in the chair shifts. His chest rises. The effort of the small movement is visible.

LEO'S FATHER
(slowly, low)
I'm here my son.

He said it. That's enough. Young Leo stays at the door.

END FLASHBACK.

Leo, older now, against the wall of the alley. He holds the dish.
He doesn't look like a boy sitting against a wall.
He looks like a boy trying not to feel what he's feeling.

He's been at this for a while.

A shadow falls across him.

He looks up.

RICHARD stands there. He is hard to age at a glance — fifties, maybe, maybe older.
He's a burly man, muscular underneath the old age. Not a typical carpenter build.
He looks at Leo the way you look at a calculation, not a person. His eyes go to the dish.

RICHARD
That dish.

What do you want for it?

Leo looks at him. Looks at the dish. He has heard of this man.
The village has opinions about him that are not comfortable.

LEO
Are you sure?

Richard's expression doesn't change. But he shifts his weight very slightly — the start of turning away.

Leo stands up fast.

LEO (CONT'D)
Wait. Yes. I—

He holds the dish out.

Richard takes three coins from his coat and holds them out. A fair price for what the dish actually is.
Not a generous price, not an insulting one.
He takes the dish directly from Leo's hands. His fingers are rough, darkened in places, like the work on them never fully comes off.

That's it. He turns and walks.

LEO (CONT'D)
Thank you.

Richard doesn't stop. Doesn't look back. The lane swallows him.

Leo looks down at the coins in his palm.

He runs.


EXT. BREAD MARKET — LATER

The bread section of the market is louder.
A vendor pulls fresh loaves out of an iron box and the smell carries.
A small queue has formed. Leo joins it and waits.

He has time for the first time today. He stands and watches the square.


FLASHBACK — EXT. MARKET SQUARE — YEARS EARLIER

The same square but fuller. Louder. The light is better. The stalls have more on them.

LEO'S FATHER — healthy, upright, wide-shouldered — moves through the crowd with easy confidence.
He is greeting people by name, stopping to laugh at something a vendor says, picking up an item from a stall and holding it up for inspection.
He takes up space in a way that doesn't feel aggressive.
He takes up space because the world is good and there is room.

LEO'S MOTHER is beside him. She is looking at something in a stall with the serious focus of someone deciding whether to spend money.
She doesn't buy it. She sets it back with a small smile.

Young LEO has hold of his father's sleeve. His father is not looking at him but his free hand comes down and settles on the boy's head briefly.
Not a look, not a word. Just a hand.

END FLASHBACK.

Present-day Leo, at the back of the bread queue. The line has moved and he hasn't. He steps forward.


EXT. BREAD MARKET — CONTINUOUS

A sound from the far side of the square. Not a loud sound.
Wrong.

Leo looks.

A MAN has a WOMAN by the arm. People nearby have pulled back.
The man's face is wrong — something happening in it that doesn't track. Not anger. Something past anger.

He twists her arm. She cries out.

Then he starts to beat her.

BYSTANDERS close in from two sides. They grab the man and pull.
He goes with them for a moment — then his body changes.
He breaks out of the grip with a strength that surprises everyone and lunges at Leo.

A CIVILIAN MAN shoves Leo hard sideways. The MAN crashes into the bread stall. Wood splinters. The vendor scrambles back.

The attacker pins the shop owner to the wreckage and is hitting him. Repeatedly. Without speaking.

The crowd surges in. It takes six people and a real effort to hold the man down.
He keeps fighting them even on the ground.

Silence settles slowly over the square. People look at each other. No one says what it was.

Leo has his back against a wall. He is breathing fast. The bread vendor's stall is destroyed.
The bread is scattered across the ground.

He is not thinking about the bread.

He goes.


EXT. VILLAGE LANES — CONTINUOUS

Leo moving fast. Not quite running. His body is doing the thinking.
He takes the turns back to his house without deciding to.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — NIGHT

The house is cold. Leo has been here for hours and has not eaten.

He sits on the floor with his back against the wall. His arms are around his knees.
He forgot the bread. He remembers now. He sits with that.

Rain starts. Heavy. It hits the roof hard.

He gets up. He's going out. He doesn't have a clear reason but he has to do something or the walls will close.

He takes two steps toward the door and his legs stop working.

Not a dramatic fall — he just goes down.
His knees hit the floor first, then his side.
He lies on the floor of his house and doesn't get up. The rain is loud.

He is very hungry.


DREAM — DARKNESS

Then: a light.

A crystal. Real and present, the way only dream-objects can be.
Held in two hands, offered upward.
The hands belong to a FIGURE in armor — distinct armor, like from the opening battlefield but whole, assembled, worn by someone who belongs in it.

The crystal is taken by a GENERAL on horseback.
A mounted figure. A hand reaching down to receive it.

The general turns his face.

The general is looking at Leo.

The light goes out.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — MORNING

Leo is on the floor. The rain has stopped. The light is gray and early.

He opens his eyes. His face is against the boards.
There is a cut on his cheek — from the fall, or from something he doesn't remember.
His ribs ache when he breathes.

He stays down for a moment.
Then he presses himself up. His arms shake slightly with it.
He sits up and looks at the room.

The light through the shutter is slow.

He is still very hungry.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

He sees it before he fully registers it.

The dish — his father's fancy useless dish, the one Richard took — is back on the shelf.
He stares at it. That is not where it was. That cannot be where it is.

He crosses to it and picks it up.

On the dish: bread. A solid loaf. Still holding warmth.

Leo stands there for a moment. He looks at the bread. He looks at the dish. He looks at the door.

He eats. Standing, both hands on the loaf, no ceremony.

When he is done he is still holding the dish.
He looks at it differently now. Someone was in this house.
Someone came in while he was on the floor and left this.

He thinks about who does that without announcing it.

He thinks about the man in the lane. Coins handed over without a word.
Dish taken directly from his hands.

Leo sets the dish down. He goes to the small chest. Opens it.
Takes out the jewelry box.

He holds it for a moment. The choice hurts more than he expected. He is not just carrying something valuable out of the house. He is carrying the last object that still feels like his mother stayed behind in some tangible form.

Then he wraps it in a cloth and tucks it under his arm.

He owes someone. He goes to pay it.


EXT. VILLAGE — DAY

Leo in the lanes. Moving with purpose for the first time.
He's looking for Richard's house — carpenter, the village says.
Feared, the village says. Leo has a debt that needs settling.


EXT. VILLAGE — DAY — CONTINUOUS

MARSHE (O.S.)
Hey.

Leo stops.

He turns slowly. He already knows.

MARSHE is at the end of the lane. Older than Leo by a couple of years and bigger by more than that.
He is looking at Leo the way someone looks at a problem they have been thinking about for a while.

Leo calculates the distance.

He runs.


EXT. VILLAGE — CONTINUOUS

The chase is not long. Marshe is not faster than Leo but the village is small and the lanes don't cooperate.
Leo rounds a corner and there is wall. He doubles back. There stands Marshe.

Leo is cornered.


EXT. ALLEY — CONTINUOUS

Marshe walks the last ten feet slowly.
He is not out of breath.
He looks at Leo the way a person looks at something they have been waiting on.

MARSHE
Where do you think you're going?

Leo doesn't answer.

Marshe hits him in the stomach first. Leo folds.
The next one comes fast to the face. Then another. A kick that drops him to the ground.

What follows is measured. Marshe is not berserk.
He is deliberate.
There is history in it — not a stranger's violence but something specific, aimed, personal.
It has Leo's name on it.

A WOMAN'S VOICE from an upper window, sharp and reprimanding.

Marshe steps back. He spits near Leo. He looks at him for a moment. He leaves.

Leo is on the ground. He stays there a moment. Then he gets up.


EXT. VILLAGE LANES — LATER

Leo limping home. Every movement hurts.

He moves without hurrying. There is nowhere to hurry to.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — AFTERNOON

Leo sits on a stool. He has cleaned the cut on his face, poorly.
He is looking at the floor.

He is holding still the way a person holds still when thinking about something they can't stop thinking about.


FLASHBACK — EXT. VILLAGE LANE — YEARS EARLIER

Two men face each other in the lane outside Leo's house.
Both are large. The tone between them is past the point of words.

MARSHE'S FATHER says something. Inaudible.
The distance and the memory compress the sound.
His posture says he has wanted this for a long time.

LEO'S FATHER says something back. Quiet. Not backing up.

Marshe's father swings first.

Leo's father moves out of the way the way water moves around a stone — without effort, without drama, already somewhere else before the fist arrives. His body responds before the moment, which makes the whole thing look like he wasn't standing there at all.

He counters. The counter is precise and fast. There is nothing improvised about it.

The fight doesn't last long. It ends when Marshe's father is on the ground and Leo's father is standing above him.

Across the lane: a younger MARSHE, perhaps seven or eight, watching from a doorway.
His face is doing too many things at once. He's looking at his father on the ground.

END FLASHBACK.

Present day. Leo in the house.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

A sound from outside. A knock, too hard to be a knock — a rock hitting the wall.

Then another.

Leo goes to the shuttered window without opening it. He listens.

Voices outside. Two of them, maybe three. Another rock.

He knows the voices without needing to place them.

He stays away from the shutter. He stays low.

Another rock. Then silence. Laughter. Moving off.

Then: a different voice. Older. A NEIGHBOR WOMAN, authoritative and terse.
The laughter cuts short. Footsteps going away.

Leo waits until the lane is quiet.

He stays where he is for a while after that.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — LATER

He finds a long pole leaning in the corner. Handle of a tool, worn smooth.

He takes it to the center of the room and tries to hold it the way his father used to hold a blade.
There is an image in his memory — partial, not quite fixed — of his father moving with a weapon. The looseness of it. The ease.

Leo tries.

He is clumsy. He knows he is clumsy. He tries to correct for the clumsiness and overcorrects and nearly drops the pole.

He resets. Tries a different hold.

Same result.

He tries a movement — a pass, a shift of his feet — and falls forward. Has to catch himself against the wall.

He stands there with his hand against the wall and the pole hanging at his side.


FLASHBACK — INT. ANOTHER ROOM — YEARS EARLIER

A different room, through a gap in a curtain. Young Leo, maybe ten, crouched on the stairs.

Through the gap: LEO'S FATHER, alone, with a sword.

He is moving with it. Not practicing. Something more native than practice.
The style doesn't follow the logic of anything Leo has seen since. It isn't formal. It isn't performed.
The weapon moves through positions that feel like they belong to his body rather than to a system — loose at the shoulder, weight low and shifting, the sword's edge always oriented somewhere that seems obvious only after the fact.

He's not fighting anything.
But watching him move, you'd think there was something real in front of him.

Young Leo watches through the gap without blinking.

END FLASHBACK.

Leo in the present room, hand still against the wall.

He sets the pole down carefully. Leans it back in the corner.

He sits on the bed.


INT. LEO'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

He sits for a long time.

His father's door is at the end of the short corridor.
He can see it from here.
It's been closed since the last weeks of the illness.
He has not opened it.

He looks at it.

He could. There is no one stopping him.
The house is his now by default and absence and nothing else.
He could walk down that corridor, turn the handle, and open it.

He doesn't.

Out of respect for his father.

He looks at the door until looking at it costs too much, and then he looks away.

Something comes loose in his thinking when he does.

He looks down.

His hands.

He looks at his hands and he sees the jewelry box is not in them, and it should be, because he brought it back from the alley and—

He looks at the floor. At the table. At the shelf.

LEO
No.

He stands. Checks the corners of the room. Moves fast through the short corridor. Nothing.

He sits down.

He sits with that for a moment. Sits with what the box is.
The last piece. The only piece. Not a pawnable thing — he refused to pawn it — but the thing he carries because she carried it and now nothing else of hers is left.

He stands up. He goes out.


EXT. VILLAGE — LATE AFTERNOON

Leo moving at a controlled pace.
The light has gone sideways — long and gold, getting shorter.
He takes the back lanes he knows.

He retraces the morning. The route to the market. The side street where he stood against the wall. The lane near the bread stalls.

Nothing.

He doubles back. Checks the gap between two buildings he passed through quickly. A stretch of ground near the square.

Nothing.

He stands in the lane and thinks.

He has been everywhere except one place.

He looks in the direction of the alley. He looks away.

He tries one more lane. Moves along it slowly, watching the ground.

Nothing.

By the time he gives up, the panic has burned down into something heavier. He sinks against a wall and sits there for a while, staring at the dirt.

There is nowhere left except the place he does not want to go.

When he finally stands, it feels less like courage than surrender.

He turns toward the alley.


He stops at the entrance.

He looks down it.

Empty. He can see to the far end.

He doesn't go in.

He circles the block. Takes the long way around. Tells himself he is checking whether there's a better angle.

He makes the full loop and arrives back at the alley entrance.

Still empty.

He still doesn't go in.

He turns and walks away.

He gets about a block before he stops. Stands there.

He turns around.


EXT. ALLEY — CONTINUOUS

Leo walks in like he has decided something.

He moves the length of the alley, scanning the ground. There is rubbish here. A broken crate. He moves it with his foot.

Nothing.

He keeps going. He is thorough. He checks the edges, the dark corners, the places things get kicked.

He almost misses it.

The box is under the broken crate, half-covered by a piece of board, lid open, one hinge bent.
It looks like something accidentally kicked and then forgotten.

His breath leaves him in a broken rush. He crosses to it quickly, dropping to a knee, his hands already reaching.
For an instant, joy breaks through his face, sudden and unguarded.

He lifts the lid.

Empty.

The feeling does not vanish all at once. It drains out of him.

He stays crouched there, staring into the hollow inside. He knew. He was already adjusting himself to know before he opened it. But knowing and seeing are different things.

He closes the lid. Stands.

He tucks the box under his arm. The wood is worn smooth in the places his mother must have touched most.

It's still the box.

He turns and walks the other way.


EXT. BACK LANE — LATE AFTERNOON

A longer route home. Quieter streets. The last of the light is going flat.

Leo is walking with the box under his arm and the day pressing down on him when he slows.

Something is wrong.

Not wrong exactly.

Warm.

He steps back and then forward again. The same spot.

Heat presses faintly through the soles of his feet. Not enough to burn. Enough to notice. He steps off it. The warmth disappears. Back on. There again.

He looks at the house.

He has walked past it before. He is sure of this.
Carpenter, the village says. Feared, the village says.
He thought about it this morning when he was going to look for Richard.

He looks down the lane. Empty in both directions.

He looks back at the house.


EXT. RICHARD'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

He moves along the wall with his hand trailing against the stone until he finds it — a gap between the shutter and the frame.
Barely wide enough. The warmth is stronger here.

He puts his eye to it.


INT. RICHARD'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS (THROUGH THE GAP)

The place looks empty at first. Not abandoned. Working.

Tools lie in careful order along the walls. Pieces of wood rest against the sides. A large table near the center holds partial cabinet work — unfinished but precise in a way that makes the whole room feel steadier than the village around it.

Then he notices something else.

Another table, further back. Half in shadow.

A sword lies on it. Unfinished — the blade shaped, rough-dressed along the edge, handle-stock fitted but not yet finished.

And behind it: a forge along the back wall. Coals banked, not cold. Still putting out heat.

Leo stares at it.

Weapons are banned in the village.
This is not a peripheral fact — it is one of the defining conditions of life here since before Leo was born.

He stares at the sword.


EXT. BACK LANE — CONTINUOUS

Leo pulls back. Checks the lane.

At the far end: movement.

Three figures. One of them at the center moves with the unhurried confidence of someone who has decided how the rest of the afternoon will go.

Marshe.

Not running. Not searching. Just moving in the direction of this lane.

Leo looks at the door. The shuttered door with its gap and the warmth on the other side.

He looks at Marshe.

He pushes through the door.


INT. RICHARD'S HOUSE — CONTINUOUS

Dark. Close. The residual heat from the forge presses in from the far wall.

Leo puts his back against the door and pulls it shut and holds it and doesn't move.

Through the wood: footsteps. Voices. Marshe and the others, moving down the lane. Their pace doesn't change.
Their voices stay casual.

The footsteps get louder. Slower.

Leo does not breathe.

The footsteps continue. Fade. Gone.

He exhales through his nose.

His eyes find the shape of the room in the low light.
The workbench. The tools in their rows.
The forge still amber at its center. The unfinished sword.

He should leave. He knows this. He should leave right now.

RICHARD (O.S.)
Step away from the door.

Leo goes still. Not the stillness of someone choosing to hold still.
The stillness of someone whose body decided before they could.

He turns.

Richard stands in the interior doorway.
He is looking at Leo the way someone looks at a problem that has not yet been categorized. Not anger. Not alarm.
Just presence, and assessment.

The two of them in the room.

Richard's eyes drop to the jewelry box in Leo's arm. Come back up to Leo's face.

He doesn't ask about it.

RICHARD (CONT'D)
You came from the alley.

It isn't a question.

Leo says nothing.

Richard crosses the room in four even steps — not toward Leo, toward the shutter.
He checks the lane through the gap.
Whatever he sees satisfies him. He closes the shutter and turns back.

He looks at Leo for a long moment.

                                RICHARD (CONT'D)
You're hurt.

A statement. An observation placed in the space between them like something that must be acknowledged before anything else can proceed.

The room is quiet.

Outside: nothing. The lane empty. The afternoon going dark.

Leo stands with the empty jewelry box under his arm and nowhere left to go.

                                                           CUT TO:


                                END OF ACT ONE (SO FAR)
