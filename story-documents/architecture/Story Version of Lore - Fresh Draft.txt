Leo wakes choking on war.

Steel screams. Men crash into each other in a blur of armor and mud. He sees it through his own eyes, yet not his own body. A blade swings too close. Someone shouts a command he cannot understand. Blood hits the ground like rain. Bodies crash past him from every side. The whole thing is motion, noise, and panic without shape or mercy.

Leo jolts upright.

The room is silent. Small. Bare. His breath comes fast, chest tight, sweat clinging to his skin. The war is gone, replaced by the quiet rot of his home. Morning light leaks through the cracks in the wood. For a moment he just sits there, listening, making sure nothing followed him out of the dream.

Nothing ever does. But it always feels like it might.

Outside this house, people call the village cursed. Inside it, the word that matters more is hunger.

Leo stands and moves through the house with slow, practiced steps. There is little to search, but he searches anyway. Habit more than hope. Drawers, corners, shelves. All empty or nearly so.

He stops at a small box.

He already knows what's inside, but he opens it anyway.

Jewelry. Old. Worn, but still delicate. His mother's.

For a second, the house fills with something else. A memory. Her voice, soft and steady, humming as she held him close. The warmth of it lingers just long enough to hurt. Leo's hand hovers over the pieces. He could take them. He could try.

He closes the box.

Not that. Not yet.

Instead, he finds a dish. Decorative, out of place in a house stripped to essentials. He turns it in his hands, weighing it like it might suddenly reveal its worth.

It won't. But it's something.

Outside, the village moves like it always does. People keep their distance without looking like they are. Doors stay closed longer than they should. Conversations die when others pass too close. Nobody wants to look too closely at anyone for too long. The air carries something heavier than silence.

Leo goes from stall to stall.

Each answer is the same, even when the words change.

Not worth it.  
Too common.  
Try somewhere else.

By the third rejection, the looks matter more than the words. Pity, irritation, suspicion. He leaves before they can say more.

He ends up sitting against a wall, the dish resting beside him. For a moment, he just stares at nothing.

Then the memory comes.

His father, sitting in the dim light of their home. Pale. Still. Fighting something that didn't care how strong he was. Leo remembers calling to him, unsure if he would respond. And then, after a long pause, his father did. Slow. Strained. But present.

Even then, he hadn't seemed weak. Just distant. Like he was somewhere else, holding the line against something no one else could see. And even in that distance, Leo remembers the way his father's attention bent back toward him first. Even while disappearing, he had kept trying to remain a father.

A shadow falls over Leo.

Richard.

The man looks at the dish, then at Leo.

"I'll buy it."

Leo blinks up at him. "Are you sure?"

Richard's face tightens almost at once, as if the question itself were already too much. He starts to turn away.

"He-hey, wait!"

Richard stops. He turns back, reaches into his coat, presses a few coins into Leo's hand, and takes the dish from Leo at the same time. His fingers are rough, darkened in places, like the work on them never fully comes off.

No negotiation. No explanation.

He turns and walks away without another word.

"Thanks!" Leo calls after him, already pushing himself up.

Then he runs home.

At the market, the smell of fresh bread cuts through everything else. Leo stops near a stall, watching as loaves are pulled from heat, steam rising into the cool air. People gather, exchanging coins for food with quiet urgency.

He waits.

And while he waits, the world shifts again.

The same place, but brighter. Louder. Alive. His mother laughing. His father standing tall, unbroken, speaking with someone across the stall. There is ease in him. Warmth. He looks like the kind of man everyone knows. Leo is smaller there, looking up at both of them, feeling like the world is stable and whole.

Then it's gone.

A scream tears through the present.

A man lunges at a woman nearby, knocking her to the ground. The crowd freezes for half a second too long, then chaos breaks loose. People shout, scramble, back away. The man doesn't stop. He strikes again, wild and uncontrolled.

Someone tackles him. Others pile on, trying to hold him down. It almost works.

Then he breaks free.

He charges blindly, straight toward Leo.

Leo doesn't move in time. Someone slams into him from the side, knocking him out of the path. The world flips. He hits the ground hard, the breath leaving his body. Behind him, wood splinters as the man crashes into the stall.

The bread vendor barely has time to react before the man is on him. Fists come down wild and brutal, driving him into the wreck of the stand.

Only then do enough people rush in again. They drag the man down by force, pinning him beneath sheer weight and numbers.

Leo doesn't stay to see more.

He runs.

By the time he reaches home, the bread is gone from his hands. He doesn't remember dropping it. His face hurts where he hit the ground. His pulse still refuses to slow. The house feels colder than before. Smaller.

Night comes heavy.

Rain pounds against the roof. Leo lies awake, hunger gnawing at him, thoughts circling without rest. Every time he closes his eyes, he sees the man breaking loose. The woman on the ground. The way the crowd backed away before it rushed in. At some point, he forces himself up. He needs food. He needs something.

He barely makes it to the door before the world tilts.

The floor rushes up to meet him.

Darkness.

Another dream.

A crystal, glowing faintly, pressed into someone's hand. A mounted figure again. Authority, weight, presence. The figure turns, and once more, looks directly at Leo. Not through him. At him.

Morning comes quietly.

Leo wakes on the floor, stiff and aching. A cut burns across his face. For a moment he doesn't move. Then he sees it.

The dish.

And on it, a piece of bread.

Fresh.

He doesn't question it. He eats immediately, tearing into it like it might vanish if he waits. When it's gone, he sits there, breathing slowly, thinking.

Richard.

It had to be.

Leo stands, slower this time, and goes back to the jewelry box. This time, he doesn't hesitate as long. He picks it up, holding it tight.

If Richard gave him bread, Leo can at least offer this in return.

The choice hurts more than he expected. He is not just carrying something valuable out of the house. He is carrying the last object that still feels like his mother stayed behind in some tangible form.

Outside, the village feels tighter than before. People speak more quietly. A few faces turn toward him, then away. News moves fast here, especially when it is ugly. Leo moves quickly, scanning for any sign of Richard.

Instead, he hears a voice.

Marshe.

Leo doesn't turn. He walks faster.

Footsteps follow. Then a shout. Then running.

Leo breaks into a sprint, weaving through narrow paths, cutting corners, trying to lose him. It doesn't work. Marshe is faster, stronger, more used to this kind of chase.

Leo hits a dead end.

He turns just as Marshe closes the distance.

Marshe slows.

"Where do you think you're going?"

He comes forward at an easy pace, like there's nowhere else Leo could possibly go.

The first hit is a punch to the stomach. It folds Leo in on himself. The next one comes fast to the face and sends him staggering. Then another. A kick that drops him to the ground. Leo tries to cover up, but it barely helps. The blows keep coming, measured, controlled.

Not random.

Intentional.

Personal.

Marshe is not lashing out at whoever happens to be near him. He is pressing on an old bruise and wanting Leo to feel exactly where it hurts.

A voice cuts in from somewhere nearby. A warning. Sharp, enough to interrupt.

Marshe pauses, just long enough to make a choice. He spits near Leo, mutters something under his breath, then steps back.

He leaves.

Leo stays on the ground for a moment before forcing himself up. Every movement hurts. He doesn't look at anyone as he limps home.

Inside, he sits in silence.

Time passes. Or maybe it doesn't.

Then another memory surfaces.

His father, standing across from Marshe's father. The tension between them is immediate, heavy. Words are exchanged, but Leo can't hear them. He only sees the shift.

Marshe's father swings first.

Fast.

Leo's father moves without hesitation. A slight shift, a step, and the strike misses. In the same motion, he counters. Clean. Efficient. Controlled. The fight that follows is not chaotic. It's precise. Every movement deliberate, like a language Leo doesn't understand.

Young Marshe watches from the side.

Horrified.

The memory snaps.

Back in the present, something strikes the outside wall.

Then another.

Voices. Laughter.

Leo stays still, listening as rocks hit the house. The boys shout, calling him out, mocking, provoking. He doesn't respond. He waits.

Eventually, another voice rises above theirs. Sharper. Older. The noise scatters. Footsteps retreat.

Silence returns.

Leo exhales slowly.

Later, he finds a long pole. Not a weapon. Not really. But close enough.

He stands in the center of the room and tries to remember.

The way his father moved. The rhythm. The looseness. Not stiff like the soldiers he's seen. Something else.

He mimics it.

Badly.

His footing is off. His grip is wrong. He swings too wide, then too short. He stumbles. Falls. Gets back up. Tries again.

Again.

Again.

Nothing works.

Frustration builds faster than progress. Eventually, he stops. The pole drops from his hands, clattering against the floor.

He sits on the edge of his bed, breathing hard.

Another memory comes, uninvited.

Him, younger, crouched on the stairs, watching through the gaps. His father below, alone, moving through those same strange motions. Fluid. Unpredictable. Every strike controlled, every transition seamless. It doesn't look like training. It looks like instinct refined into something sharper.

Leo hadn't understood it then.

He doesn't understand it now.

He cannot become closer to his father by copying him. The attempt only proves how far away the man still is, even in memory.

The memory fades.

Leo lifts his head.

Across the room, the door to his father's room stands closed.

He looks at it for a long time.

There are answers in there. Or at least pieces of them. But the space feels sealed. Not physically. Something else. Respect. Distance. Fear of what it might confirm.

He considers it.

Then he looks away.

Something is missing.

The thought arrives without warning, sharp enough to cut through everything else. Leo's hand moves to his side, searching for the weight he'd carried out of the house.

Nothing.

He goes still.

The jewelry box.

His mother's jewelry box.

For one disbelieving second, everything around him falls away. He sees his own hand closing the lid that morning. Hears her voice. Feels the stubborn choice he made when he refused to sell it.

Gone.

Dropped somewhere in the chase. Lost before he even understood he'd lost it.

He stays where he is, as if moving would make it fully real.

Then he moves all at once.

He is out the door before the thought can settle. Back through the same paths. Back through the turns he took while running. His eyes search the ground, every corner, every scrap near the walls.

Nothing.

He doubles back. Checks again. Faster this time. Then slower.

Still nothing.

The alley comes into view.

Leo stops.

The bruises Marshe left on him seem to wake all at once. His stomach tightens. His face still burns where he was hit. He looks toward the narrow space, then away again.

He keeps walking.

He searches the other paths first. The longer way around. Then another. He tells himself he might have dropped it anywhere while running. He checks near doorways, corners, loose stone, the edges of the lane.

Nothing.

By the time he gives up, the panic has burned down into something heavier. He sinks against a wall and sits there for a while, staring at the dirt.

There is nowhere left except the place he does not want to go.

When he finally stands, it feels less like courage than surrender.

He goes back to the alley.

At first he sees only the ground.

Then something catches his eye near the wall, farther in than where he remembers falling.

The box.

His breath leaves him in a broken rush. He crosses to it quickly, dropping to a knee, his hands already reaching. For an instant, joy breaks through his face, sudden and unguarded.

He lifts the lid.

Empty.

The feeling does not vanish all at once. It drains out of him.

The jewelry is gone.

He stays crouched there, staring into the hollow inside as if something might still appear if he waits long enough. It should feel like a total loss. Weeks of food, maybe more, gone with a few careless hands or feet.

But that is not what hits hardest.

The box is still here.

His fingers close around it, careful now. The wood is worn smooth in the places his mother must have touched most. The jewelry mattered. The hunger matters. Richard matters. All of that is real. But this surviving feels bigger than the money ever was. Some part of her made it through.

Leo closes the lid and stands.

He still has nothing to offer.

He starts back toward home, taking a different route this time. He wants no part of the square. No chance of running into Marshe again.

The village is quieter here. Fewer voices. More space between houses.

As he passes one narrow stretch beside a home, he slows.

Something feels wrong.

Not wrong exactly.

Warm.

He steps back and then forward again.

The same spot.

Heat presses faintly through the soles of his feet. Not enough to burn. Enough to notice. He steps off it. The warmth disappears. Back on. There again.

Leo looks at the house.

No smoke. No voices. No sign of anyone outside.

He moves closer, curiosity pushing past caution for a moment, and peeks through the window.

The place looks empty at first. Not abandoned. Working. Tools lie in careful disorder. Pieces of wood rest along the walls. A large table stands near the center of the room with partial cabinets spread across it, unfinished but precise in a way that makes the whole room feel steadier than the village around it.

Then he notices something else.

Another table across the room.

A sword lies on it.

Leo goes still.

It is unfinished. The grip is missing, or not yet attached, but the shape is unmistakable. He has only seen a sword up close when watching his father train. Weapons are forbidden here. Everyone knows that.

So who would keep one in the open?

He starts to pull away from the window.

Voices reach him from nearby.

Boys. Laughing.

Then one of them speaks clearly enough that Leo knows the voice at once.

Marshe.

Panic hits fast. Leo looks once toward the path, then at the house. There is no time to run without being seen.

He tries the door.

Unlocked.

He slips inside and eases it shut behind him, then crouches low beside it, barely breathing.

The voices draw nearer. Marshe and the others pass outside still talking, still loud with the kind of ease that comes after hurting someone smaller than you. Leo stays frozen until the noise begins to fade. Even then he waits.

Only when the last of it disappears into the distance does he let out a breath.

His shoulders loosen.

He turns to rise.

Someone is standing there.

Leo flinches so hard he nearly loses his footing.

For a second he sees only the back of the man, broad and still in the dim room. Then the figure turns.

Richard.
