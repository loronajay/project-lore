The kingdom at the center of LORE likes to imagine itself as ancient, holy, and inevitable.

From the outside, it presents order. Its banners are clean. Its records are stable. Its religion sounds old enough to feel immovable. Its King appears benevolent, spiritually significant, and historically justified. To many people living under his rule, that appearance is enough. The world they have been given is coherent. The kingdom descends from the ancients. Each generation is taught to expect a modern ancient fit to rule. The throne is treated not just as power, but as sacred continuation.

What makes the lie durable is that it is built on truth.

The ancients were real. Their bloodline matters. Their language survives in fragments and materials that still resist full understanding. Religion in this world is not empty fabrication. It is a damaged inheritance. People believe it because enough of it is real to hold.

But the deepest part of that belief has been buried.

Hidden doctrine says the true prophesied figure should be able to read the ancients' language at a glance. The current King cannot. He knows he cannot. That single fact sits beneath everything he has built. He suppresses it, then builds an entire system around keeping that truth contained.

That system hardened over time. Before his reign, priests alone were permitted direct access to surviving ancient material. Later the King widened that access to selected researchers and historians, then gradually narrowed it again as control became more important than scholarship. Now only he and a tight propaganda inner circle are allowed to work from the real material directly. Everyone else studies curated findings, managed interpretations, and useful discoveries already shaped for political survival.

This is one of the reasons the kingdom remains stable.

The broader religious and scholarly institution is not fully let in on the lie. Most of it is still being deceived, even if that margin is narrowing over time. Those who push too hard against the King's narrative are silenced, exiled, or killed. Those who remain useful are given just enough to defend the public story without ever being allowed to test it at the source.

His legitimacy is therefore not only political theater. It is a sustained act of theft.

Long before Leo was born, the royal family had many children. But knowledge about the ancients gene did not move cleanly into public life. The question of which royal child, if any, truly carried it remained something ordinary people understood only through fragments, rumor, and delayed official narrative. Inside the castle, the matter was tracked more closely. The older children had not emerged as confirmed bearers. Then, late in life, their parents had one more child: Rowan. He was internally confirmed as the real bearer. Under the kingdom's own deeper logic, that made him the strongest legitimate claimant to the throne.

By then, the older brother who would become King had already been poisoned from the inside.

Thorne had spent years placing spies close enough to him to shape his thinking. They convinced him he had been lied to, that he was the true bearer, and that he was destined to find the crystal. So when Rowan was confirmed inside the castle, he moved fast. He arranged the deaths of his parents and siblings and seized power before that truth could spread outward in a form the public could absorb.

Rowan survived only as an infant. He was not spared out of mercy. He was spared because another discovery had surfaced behind the walls of the castle: the stronger prophesied figure should be able to read the ancients' language at a glance. The baby might therefore prove useful, especially in connection to another obsession growing quietly behind the throne: the crystal.

After taking power, the King did not simply rule. He rewrote.

Birth records were erased. Witnesses were silenced. History was revised. Religion was narrowed, repackaged, and made politically useful. Rowan was stripped of his royal identity and renamed Jack — a deliberately plain name, a quiet act of domination. He was reintroduced as an orphaned commoner the King had supposedly taken in out of kindness. He grew up believing that lie. He was grateful for it. He loved the King as a benefactor and father figure, and by the time he became a soldier, the false version of his life already felt ordinary.

That buried relationship is one of the kingdom's deepest fractures.

Jack is not only the rightful claimant in a technical sense. He is the living contradiction of the King's public holiness. He is proof that the current order is not merely harsh or corrupt, but fundamentally stolen.

Gideon's response to that danger was never limited to one act of violence. He reorganized reality around the theft. Public memory was trained to accept a cleaner version of succession than the real one. That is why the kingdom does not experience his reign as a messy coup everyone secretly knows was illegitimate. It experiences his rule as order.

That same pattern appears in the kingdom's treatment of exile.

At the kingdom's edge sits the village where Leo grows up. It is not merely poor. It is engineered to remain vulnerable. It houses exiles and their descendants, people the kingdom wants kept separate, weakened, and easy to blame. Weapons are forbidden there under the exile contract. Royal soldiers can search the village during audits. Its people are left isolated enough that fear grows faster than truth.

The village also lives under a curse story.

People believe a warlock's curse hangs over the place and causes the sickness spreading through its homes. That belief gives suffering a shape. It turns fear into folklore. But the sickness is real whether or not the curse is. It causes deterioration, loss of control, violence, and death. Families hide the infected. Some die in private. Some kill loved ones before anyone outside understands what happened. The town's public terror is only the visible edge of a much larger private collapse.

Meanwhile, beyond the village, the King spends years studying the crystal.

The crystal is real. It is tied somehow to awakening or reaching the ancients. Gideon wants it, and Thorne seeks it too. But Thorne is not simply a distant rival with the same goal. The two realms were once one older kingdom, split apart long ago by a conflict between brothers. Dorian, king of Thorne, is in fact the uncle of Gideon. Thorne's sabotage therefore does not come from a cleanly foreign distance. It grows out of an old dynastic fracture that has been hardening for decades. It has also spent decades studying Eden's religion, history, and succession logic in hopes of reaching the crystal without needing a large war. Its sabotage helped shape the very throne now pursuing the same object.

That desperation is not self-contained.

Thorne is under pressure from the same larger force that wants the ancients' history erased from the world. That force appears to be a former enemy of the ancients, now weakened enough that it can no longer dominate wide parts of the world directly. Instead it works through individuals, bargains, and corruption. Dorian has already made such a bargain. In exchange for the crystal, he was promised restored youth, and the bargain was sweetened by granting that youth early. Though he is in truth an old man, he now appears as a strong young noble, better equipped in body and stature to prepare for the taking of the crystal.

Even so, the surviving ancient material is hard to read cleanly. Meaning remains trapped behind partial knowledge. This is the same pressure point that haunts the throne itself: proximity to truth without the power to fully recognize it.

Eventually, those political and spiritual anxieties converge.

There is another exiled village. The King suspects it may be planning rebellion. Around the same time, his researchers begin to fear that the crystal could be nearer than they thought. Fear and opportunity combine. He gives an order that reveals what his rule actually is beneath its polished surface.

Jack and Marcus are both high-ranking members of the royal army by then. Richard belongs to that same military world and had risen to General before Jack later replaced him. Jack is battle-tested, respected, and still serving Gideon with full loyalty because he has no reason yet to understand the lie beneath his life. Gideon orders the mission: wipe out the other exiled village, erase its history, and retrieve the crystal.

Richard's position inside that world is more complicated.

He is not just another servant of the throne. He is one of the King's most useful and dangerous assets, trusted with more proximity to hidden power than most men ever receive. He knows enough to see that some things are wrong. He notices doctrinal drift. He sees violence, concealment, and the moral rot beneath the public image. But he still does not know the whole structure behind what he is serving. He does not know how the King truly took power. He does not know that Jack is Gideon's younger brother. He does not yet understand the deeper motive behind the King's pursuit of the crystal beyond the prophetic promise attached to it.

Jack refuses.

Once he understands the truth of the order, he aborts the mission and returns. Marcus wants to obey. That moment becomes one of the clearest moral fractures in the backstory. It is not just a disagreement about command. It is the point where obedience, legitimacy, history, and conscience split apart.

The King's response is merciless and careful at the same time.

He exiles the entire squadron publicly, then secretly executes everyone except Jack and Marcus. The survivors are not merely cast out. They are discredited. Rumors are spread to poison the public story around them so the exile of such revered soldiers can be made to seem justified. What cannot be cleanly erased is made shameful instead.

After the exile, the King turned to a problem he had been deferring: an heir. Still without one, he chose a woman in the court and forced her to bear his child. He wanted an heir he could shape from birth. He believed the child was his. She was Esmeralda. He was disappointed she was a daughter, but not enough to discard her.

What he did not know was that the woman had quietly been in a relationship with Richard — a secret Richard had kept carefully concealed from the throne. Richard is Esmeralda's biological father. The King does not know this. So even the child he believes secures his line is bound to the same deeper instability that haunts everything else in his rule: a public story built on a hidden truth that would undo it.

When Esmeralda was still very young, Richard and her mother planned to flee together. Richard pulled back at the last moment, deciding that exposure would get them all killed and that the castle was safer for a child than exile. Her mother did not listen. She proceeded alone, was caught, and was executed. Richard has lived since with the knowledge that his withdrawal is part of why she acted without him.

Over time the King became genuinely attached to Esmeralda — possessively, sincerely, and without moral transformation. She became the adored daughter of a beloved ruler and the shining future of the kingdom. Privately, she became another place where tenderness and brutality occupied the same person at once.

But there was a problem the inner circle knew and the public did not. Esmeralda does not carry the ancients gene. The same tight circle that knew the suppressed doctrinal marker knew this too. It meant the heir he was building failed the kingdom's deeper standard of legitimacy.

Rather than raise another child, the King adapted. The religion already carried a future-facing prophecy: a stronger figure would one day lead the people back to the ancients. He claimed Esmeralda as its fulfillment — not by rewriting the prophecy, but by positioning her inside it. His inner circle produced convenient doctrinal alignments, staged interpretive discoveries, and useful readings of ancient material that allowed the existing prophecy to apply to her. He raised her inside that claim emotionally as well. From childhood she was given a story about a princess who would one day save the world from itself. By the time she was old enough to act within the system, the prophecy already felt personal, sacred, and self-evidently true.

For Richard, the truth about Esmeralda became one of the sharpest private punishments of his life. He knew who she was. He believed that exposing the truth would get them both killed and break open a terror he had no power to contain. So he chose distance, watched from afar, and let her grow up inside the King's false fatherhood while telling himself silence was the only way to keep her alive.

Shortly after Esmeralda's birth, the King decided that Richard's proximity to too much hidden truth had become its own danger. He removed him through exile as well — not as part of the original refusal, but as another act of containment.

The entity had already taken notice. Once Thorne, Dorian, and the entity learned that Jack's bloodline was present in the exile village, the entity introduced the sickness deliberately — a subtle instrument of elimination that could work without open war and without leaving a visible hand behind it. The village had no way to understand what was happening to it. The warlock's curse story was not entirely wrong about the nature of the threat. It was only wrong about who was responsible.

That is how the kingdom keeps its shape.

It does not rely on force alone. It uses records, doctrine, selective truth, social isolation, and fear. It turns villages into holding pens. It turns sickness into superstition. It turns rightful blood into buried rumor. It turns the fall of revered men into a story the public can accept.

And still, the structure is unstable.

Richard eventually uncovers enough of the hidden truth to understand that Jack was never just a disgraced soldier. He was the living contradiction at the center of Gideon's rule: the true heir, hidden inside a counterfeit life. More than that, he was a genuinely good man built inside a false story. Richard begins preparing for a rebellion meant not only to oppose the King, but to restore what was stolen.

Jack resists at first, not because the truth is meaningless, but because accepting it would force him to risk Leo's future and his own life. By the time he finally begins to accept that buried history, the sickness destroys the path ahead. He falls ill, grows distant, and the rebellion dies with him.

Richard's silence before that point is part of his tragedy. He is loyal to the people of the kingdom more than he is ever loyal to the King. He says nothing about many of the distortions he notices because he believes half-truth without remedy would only bring panic and fear to ordinary people already living inside a fragile order. Later, once he understands enough to connect doctrine, royalty, and the crystal into a coherent theft, he stops treating silence as service and starts treating action as duty. Many of the losses that follow are things he accepts almost as punishment for having once looked away, even though he did so in what he believed was the people's interest.

That logic touches Esmeralda too. His silence about her is not separate from the rest of his compromise. It is one of the clearest places where private love, political fear, and protective self-denial collapse into the same choice.

But the larger problem remains.

So the kingdom now rests on multiple false answers at once: the King's own stolen authority, the daughter's manufactured destiny built from a prophecy that already existed and was simply claimed for her, and the buried conviction that the real line has already been erased.

He does not yet understand how wrong that is.

Leo lives inside the aftermath of all of this without knowing it.

He grows up in the exile village shaped by the kingdom's fear, its lies, its religion, and its violence. He inherits hunger on the surface and buried history underneath. The kingdom believes it solved its succession problem long ago. In truth, it only delayed it, buried it, and forced it to grow in the dark.
