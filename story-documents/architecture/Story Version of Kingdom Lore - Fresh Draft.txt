The kingdom at the center of LORE likes to imagine itself as ancient, holy, and inevitable.

From the outside, it presents order. Its banners are clean. Its records are stable. Its religion sounds old enough to feel immovable. Its King appears benevolent, spiritually significant, and historically justified. To many people living under his rule, that appearance is enough. The world they have been given is coherent. The kingdom descends from the ancients. Each generation is taught to expect a modern ancient fit to rule. The throne is treated not just as power, but as sacred continuation.

What makes the lie durable is that it is built on truth.

The ancients were real. Their bloodline matters. Their language survives in fragments and materials that still resist full understanding. Religion in this world is not empty fabrication. It is a damaged inheritance. People believe it because enough of it is real to hold.

But the deepest part of that belief has been buried.

Hidden doctrine says the true prophesied figure should be able to read the ancients' language at a glance. The current King cannot. He knows he cannot. That single fact sits beneath everything he has built. He suppresses it, then builds an entire system around keeping that truth contained.

That system hardened over time. Before his reign, priests alone were permitted direct access to surviving ancient material. Later the King widened that access to selected researchers and historians, then gradually narrowed it again as control became more important than scholarship. Now only he and a tight propaganda inner circle are allowed to work from the real material directly. Everyone else studies curated findings, managed interpretations, and useful discoveries already shaped for political survival.

This is one of the reasons the kingdom remains stable.

The broader religious and scholarly institution is not fully let in on the lie. Most of it is still being deceived, even if that margin is narrowing over time. Those who push too hard against the King's narrative are silenced, exiled, or killed. Those who remain useful are given just enough to defend the public story without ever being allowed to test it at the source.

His legitimacy is therefore not only political theater. It is a sustained act of theft.

Long before Leo was born, the royal family had many children. None of them were confirmed to carry the ancients gene. Then, late in life, their parents had one more child: Leo's father. He was the only confirmed bearer. Under the kingdom's own deeper logic, that made him the strongest legitimate claimant to the throne. The older brother who would become King understood exactly what that meant.

So he moved first.

He arranged the deaths of his parents and siblings and seized power before the truth could settle into public life. Leo's father survived only as an infant. He was not spared out of mercy. He was spared because the ancients gene might still prove useful, especially in connection to another obsession growing quietly behind the throne: the crystal.

After taking power, the King did not simply rule. He rewrote.

Birth records were erased. Witnesses were silenced. History was revised. Religion was narrowed, repackaged, and made politically useful. Leo's father was stripped of his royal identity and reintroduced as an orphaned commoner the King had supposedly taken in out of kindness. He grew up believing that lie. He was grateful for it. He loved the King as a benefactor and father figure, and by the time he became a soldier, the false version of his life already felt ordinary.

That buried relationship is one of the kingdom's deepest fractures.

Leo's father is not only the rightful claimant in a technical sense. He is the living contradiction of the King's public holiness. He is proof that the current order is not merely harsh or corrupt, but fundamentally stolen.

The King's response to that danger was never limited to one act of violence. He reorganized reality around the theft. Public memory was trained to accept a cleaner version of succession than the real one. That is why the kingdom does not experience his reign as a messy coup everyone secretly knows was illegitimate. It experiences his rule as order.

But Leo's father was not the only place where the King's stolen legitimacy would eventually fracture.

Years later, still trying to secure the future of his line, the King forced a woman to bear him a child. He wanted an heir he could shape from birth. He believed the child was his. She was Esmeralda. He was disappointed she was a daughter, but not enough to discard her. When her mother later tried to flee with her, he had the mother executed and raised the girl himself.

The same pattern repeated in a new form: coercion, possession, reinvention, and a false story built tightly enough to pass as truth.

Over time, something more complicated grew inside that arrangement. The King became genuinely attached to Esmeralda. He spoiled her. He loved her in the way a man like him is capable of loving: possessively, sincerely, and without moral transformation. Publicly, she became the adored daughter of a beloved ruler and the shining future of the kingdom. Privately, she became another point where tenderness and brutality occupied the same person at once.

And still, even there, the bloodline beneath the surface remained unstable.

Esmeralda is not actually his daughter. Richard is her biological father. The King does not know this. The woman he forced into his dynastic plan had secretly been a love interest of Richard's after Richard lost his wife. So even the child the King believes secures his line is bound to the same deeper instability that haunts everything else in his rule: a public story of legitimacy built on a hidden truth that would undo it.

There is another fracture inside that succession story too.

Esmeralda does not carry the ancients gene. The King knows it. The same tight inner circle that knows the suppressed doctrinal truth knows it too. They keep the fact tightly controlled because once it is admitted, the problem is no longer only that the child is secretly not his. It is that even the official heir he is building cannot satisfy the kingdom's deeper standard of legitimacy.

So the King adapts.

Rather than raise another child and risk another unstable future, he reshapes prophecy again. He supports new doctrinal readings, controlled interpretations, and staged discoveries that allow Esmeralda to be framed as a chosen successor even though she fails the deeper standard. He also raises her inside that revision emotionally, not just politically. From childhood she is given a story about a princess who will one day save the world from itself. By the time she is old enough to act within the system, that false destiny already feels sacred and natural to her.

For Richard, the truth about Esmeralda becomes one of the sharpest forms of private punishment in his life.

He knows who she is. He knows she is his daughter. He also believes that exposing the truth would get them both killed and break open terror he has no power yet to contain. So he chooses distance, watches from afar, and lets her grow up inside the King's false fatherhood while telling himself that silence is the only way to keep her alive.

That same pattern appears in the kingdom's treatment of exile.

At the kingdom's edge sits the village where Leo grows up. It is not merely poor. It is engineered to remain vulnerable. It houses exiles and their descendants, people the kingdom wants kept separate, weakened, and easy to blame. Weapons are forbidden there under the exile contract. Royal soldiers can search the village during audits. Its people are left isolated enough that fear grows faster than truth.

The village also lives under a curse story.

People believe a warlock's curse hangs over the place and causes the sickness spreading through its homes. That belief gives suffering a shape. It turns fear into folklore. But the sickness is real whether or not the curse is. It causes deterioration, loss of control, violence, and death. Families hide the infected. Some die in private. Some kill loved ones before anyone outside understands what happened. The town's public terror is only the visible edge of a much larger private collapse.

Meanwhile, beyond the village, the King spends years studying the crystal.

The crystal is real. It is tied somehow to awakening or reaching the ancients. The King wants it, and another kingdom seeks it too. His researchers believe it may be closer than expected, but the surviving ancient material is hard to read cleanly. Meaning remains trapped behind partial knowledge. This is the same pressure point that haunts the throne itself: proximity to truth without the power to fully recognize it.

Eventually, those political and spiritual anxieties converge.

There is another exiled village. The King suspects it may be planning rebellion. Around the same time, his researchers begin to fear that the crystal could be nearer than they thought. Fear and opportunity combine. He gives an order that reveals what his rule actually is beneath its polished surface.

Leo's father, Richard, and Marshe's father are all high-ranking members of the royal army by then. Richard had risen to General before Leo's father later replaced him. Leo's father is battle-tested, respected, and still serving the King with full loyalty because he has no reason yet to understand the lie beneath his life. The King orders their mission: wipe out the other exiled village, erase its history, and retrieve the crystal.

Richard's position inside that world is more complicated.

He is not just another servant of the throne. He is one of the King's most useful and dangerous assets, trusted with more proximity to hidden power than most men ever receive. He knows enough to see that some things are wrong. He notices doctrinal drift. He sees violence, concealment, and the moral rot beneath the public image. But he still does not know the whole structure behind what he is serving. He does not know how the King truly took power. He does not know that Leo's father is the King's younger brother. He does not yet understand the deeper motive behind the King's pursuit of the crystal beyond the prophetic promise attached to it.

Leo's father refuses.

Once he understands the truth of the order, he aborts the mission and returns. Marshe's father wants to obey. Richard stands with Leo's father. That moment becomes one of the clearest moral fractures in the backstory. It is not just a disagreement about command. It is the point where obedience, legitimacy, history, and conscience split apart.

The King's response is merciless and careful at the same time.

He exiles the entire squadron publicly, then secretly executes everyone except Leo's father, Richard, and Marshe's father. The survivors are not merely cast out. They are discredited. Rumors are spread to poison the public story around them so the exile of such revered soldiers can be made to seem justified. What cannot be cleanly erased is made shameful instead.

That is how the kingdom keeps its shape.

It does not rely on force alone. It uses records, doctrine, selective truth, social isolation, and fear. It turns villages into holding pens. It turns sickness into superstition. It turns rightful blood into buried rumor. It turns the fall of revered men into a story the public can accept.

And still, the structure is unstable.

Richard eventually uncovers enough of the hidden truth to understand that Leo's father was never just a disgraced soldier. He was the living contradiction at the center of the King's rule: the true heir, hidden inside a counterfeit life. More than that, he was a genuinely good man built inside a false story. Richard begins preparing for a rebellion meant not only to oppose the King, but to restore what was stolen.

Leo's father resists at first, not because the truth is meaningless, but because accepting it would force him to risk Leo's future and his own life. By the time he finally begins to accept that buried history, the sickness destroys the path ahead. He falls ill, grows distant, and the rebellion dies with him.

Richard's silence before that point is part of his tragedy. He is loyal to the people of the kingdom more than he is ever loyal to the King. He says nothing about many of the distortions he notices because he believes half-truth without remedy would only bring panic and fear to ordinary people already living inside a fragile order. Later, once he understands enough to connect doctrine, royalty, and the crystal into a coherent theft, he stops treating silence as service and starts treating action as duty. Many of the losses that follow are things he accepts almost as punishment for having once looked away, even though he did so in what he believed was the people's interest.

That logic touches Esmeralda too. His silence about her is not separate from the rest of his compromise. It is one of the clearest places where private love, political fear, and protective self-denial collapse into the same choice.

But the larger problem remains.

The religion still carries a future-facing prophecy that a stronger figure will one day lead the people back to the ancients. The King has built his life around controlling the older line of legitimacy. He has also begun adapting that prophecy again to position Esmeralda as his chosen successor, even though she does not carry the ancients gene.

He does not do that only through speeches or image-making. He uses the same machinery he used to stabilize his own rule. Convenient doctrine is unveiled. Ancient texts are interpreted in newly useful ways. His inner circle helps produce a religious story the public can inhabit without feeling the manipulation inside it. By the time Esmeralda is old enough to act within the system, the lie already feels moral, sacred, and personal to her.

So the kingdom now rests on multiple false answers at once: the King's own stolen authority, the daughter's manufactured destiny, and the buried conviction that the real line has already been erased.

He does not yet understand how wrong that is.

Leo lives inside the aftermath of all of this without knowing it.

He grows up in the exile village shaped by the kingdom's fear, its lies, its religion, and its violence. He inherits hunger on the surface and buried history underneath. The kingdom believes it solved its succession problem long ago. In truth, it only delayed it, buried it, and forced it to grow in the dark.
